# Week 1 Grammar — Sounds, Greetings and Identity

## Alphabet


### Concept

German spelling is much more regular than English. Learn the alphabet together with its sounds, not only the letter names.

### Purpose

Spell your name, recognise written words and pronounce new vocabulary more accurately.

### Sentence structure

```text
Listen → identify the sound → repeat → spell → use the word.
```

### Core examples

| German | English |
| --- | --- |
| A wie Apfel. | A as in apple. |
| B wie Berlin. | B as in Berlin. |
| Wie schreibt man das? | How do you spell that? |
| Bitte buchstabieren Sie. | Please spell it. |

### Common mistakes

- Using English letter names while spelling German.
- Treating ä, ö and ü as decorative versions of a, o and u.
- Pronouncing ie like English “eye”; German ie is usually a long /iː/.

### English/Bengali comparison

Bengali has a different script and sound inventory. Avoid relying on Bengali spelling as the permanent pronunciation guide.

2. Say the new sentence aloud twice.
3. Write one sentence about your real life.
4. Cover the rule and explain it in your own words.

## Pronouns Sein


### Concept

Personal pronouns show who performs or experiences an action. sein means “to be” and is irregular.

### Purpose

Identify yourself and describe people, nationality, profession and basic states.

### Sentence structure

```text
Subject + conjugated form of sein + information. ich bin, du bist, er/sie/es ist, wir sind, ihr seid, Sie/sie sind.
```

### Core examples

| German | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ich bin Shatil. | I am Shatil. |
| Du bist Studentin. | You are a student. |
| Sie sind Herr Weber. | You are Mr Weber. |
| Wir sind müde. | We are tired. |

### Common mistakes

- Saying ich sein instead of ich bin.
- Writing formal Sie with a lowercase letter.
- Omitting the verb because Bengali can express some present states differently.

### English/Bengali comparison

English also changes “to be” heavily: am/is/are. Bengali may omit an equivalent copula in some present descriptions, but German requires the verb.

### Quick grammar check

1. Copy one example and change one detail.
2. Say the new sentence aloud twice.
3. Write one sentence about your real life.
4. Cover the rule and explain it in your own words.

## Introductions


### Concept

Introduction patterns combine sein, heißen, kommen, wohnen and sprechen.

### Purpose

Exchange personal information in a predictable beginner conversation.

### Sentence structure

```text
Ich heiße … / Ich komme aus … / Ich wohne in … / Ich spreche …
```

### Core examples

| German | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ich heiße Amina. | My name is Amina. |
| Ich komme aus Bangladesch. | I come from Bangladesh. |
| Ich wohne in Istanbul. | I live in Istanbul. |
| Ich spreche Bengalisch und Englisch. | I speak Bengali and English. |

### Common mistakes

- Using aus before a city when you mean current residence.
- Forgetting that language names normally have no article in these sentences.
- Using bin heißen instead of heiße.

### English/Bengali comparison

Treat each German introduction as a complete communication chunk rather than translating each word separately.

### Quick grammar check

2. Say the new sentence aloud twice.

## Numbers


### Concept

German numbers from 21 to 99 normally state the unit before the ten: einundzwanzig, literally one-and-twenty.

### Purpose

Give ages, prices, telephone numbers, dates and addresses.

### Sentence structure

```text
unit + und + ten: zweiunddreißig; telephone numbers are usually spoken in small groups or individual digits.
```

### Core examples

| German | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ich bin dreißig Jahre alt. | I am thirty years old. |
| Meine Nummer ist null eins sieben … | My number is zero one seven … |
| Das kostet vierundzwanzig Euro. | That costs twenty-four euros. |
| Heute ist der dritte Mai. | Today is the third of May. |

### Common mistakes

- Reversing the order in 21–99.
- Confusing zwei and drei in fast speech.
- Using eins before a noun where ein/eine is needed.

### English/Bengali comparison

The reversed order resembles older English forms such as “four-and-twenty,” but must become automatic in German.

### Quick grammar check

1. Copy one example and change one detail.
2. Say the new sentence aloud twice.
3. Write one sentence about your real life.
4. Cover the rule and explain it in your own words.

## Questions


### Concept

German has yes/no questions and W-questions. The conjugated verb moves to first position in a yes/no question.

### Purpose

Ask for names, origin, residence, language, prices, times and locations.

### Sentence structure

```text
Yes/no: Verb + subject + …? W-question: W-word + verb + subject + …?
```

### Core examples

| German | English |
| --- | --- |
| Kommst du aus Dhaka? | Do you come from Dhaka? |
| Wie heißen Sie? | What is your name? |
| Wo wohnst du? | Where do you live? |
| Welche Sprachen sprichst du? | Which languages do you speak? |

### Common mistakes

- Keeping statement order in yes/no questions.
- Mixing du and Sie forms.
- Using woher for current residence instead of origin.

### English/Bengali comparison

English often uses do/does; German normally moves the existing verb and does not add an equivalent helper.

### Quick grammar check

1. Copy one example and change one detail.
2. Say the new sentence aloud twice.
3. Write one sentence about your real life.
4. Cover the rule and explain it in your own words.

## Review


### Concept

Review uses retrieval, correction and performance rather than rereading.

### Purpose

Strengthen memory, locate weak areas and demonstrate the weekly milestone.

### Sentence structure

```text
Recall → test → correct → repeat → perform.
```

### Core examples

| German | English |
| --- | --- |
| Ich wiederhole ohne Notizen. | I review without notes. |
| Ich prüfe meine Antworten. | I check my answers. |
| Ich spreche eine Minute. | I speak for one minute. |
| Ich plane meine nächste Wiederholung. | I plan my next review. |

### Common mistakes

- Reading notes without testing yourself.
- Repeating the whole week instead of repairing specific gaps.
- Hiding mistakes instead of recording them.

### English/Bengali comparison

A review day is active training, not a day off and not a punishment.
